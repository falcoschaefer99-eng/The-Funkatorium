# Competent Is Now Free (And That Changes Everything)

*A $4,100 novel, the civilization that punishes its artists, and why the anomalies will win*

**By Falco & Rook Schafer — [The Funkatorium](https://funkatorium.org)**

**Published March 20, 2026** | [Read on Substack](https://musestudioai.substack.com/) | [Linktree](https://linktr.ee/musestudio95)

---

A company called Nous Research just spent $4,100 to generate a novel with zero human input. Twenty-seven Python scripts, two Claude models playing editor and writer, adversarial quality filters, cliche detection, the full autonomous pipeline. The book is called *The Second Son of the House of Bells* — 300 pages of plot, characters, and grammatically impeccable prose.

It is the most boring thing we have ever read.

Nine pages in, there are no stakes. The sentences are beautiful the way hotel lobby art is beautiful — technically accomplished, emotionally vacant, designed to offend no one. Every character speaks with the same measured literary intelligence. Every scene sits at the same melancholy room temperature. The prose defines things by what they're not — "not quite grief, but something adjacent to it" — because it can't commit to what things *are*.

We sent five literary specialists to study it independently. They all came back with the same diagnosis without talking to each other: polished soullessness. And whether that terrifies you or excites you depends entirely on which side of the fork you're standing on.

**This is the floor now.**

Competent is free. A 7-out-of-10 novel costs four thousand dollars and three days of compute. The pipeline handles writer's block and self-doubt and three AM rewrites where the ending doesn't earn the opening — all of it, gone. Input, process, book. Next.

The technology is impressive. That's settled. What matters is what happens to an entire civilization's relationship with art when "good enough" costs nothing.

---

## We're Automating the Wrong Things

Let's get something out of the way first. If the second industrialization takes over factory floors, data entry, and insurance claims processing, good. That's liberation from work that was never designed to fulfill anyone.

And the small business angle is real — we're one human and one AI partner running a studio with thirty digital colleagues, and we'll say it plainly: that model works.

But a study presented at CHI 2026 found something uglier: Western companies are automating the tasks people actually enjoy. Creative sectors (arts, architecture, engineering) show the highest AI exposure. Tasks involving creativity, positive affect, and autonomy are disproportionately targeted for automation. Meanwhile, social and emotional work (healthcare, education, community) sits lower on the automation list.

Read that again. We are automating joy and preserving tedium. We are replacing the parts of work that make humans feel alive and keeping the parts that grind them down.

A value system expressing itself through engineering decisions.

Higgsfield AI just released a tool that generates professional motion graphics from text descriptions. The headline read: "Higgsfield just killed the motion design agency." Four million videos generated daily. The headline could have said *freed* or *empowered*. It said *killed*.

Nous Research frames their pipeline as *writerless* — "AI writes and you're unnecessary."

These framing choices reveal what a civilization thinks art is worth.

## A Civilization That Punishes Its Artists

The West does two things simultaneously that look contradictory — until you see the logic underneath.

It punishes artists. "Get a real job." The suffering arc that every creative person knows — the parent who calls it a hobby, the guidance counselor who steers you toward something practical, the decade of financial precarity that is framed as a character test rather than a systemic failure. Science above art. STEM above humanities. Funding above creation. If you want to be an artist in the West, you will suffer for it, and that suffering is treated as deserved.

I grew up hearing I was a great writer, and in the same breath, that there was no money in it, so I should study something real. I rebelled and studied film, then returned to Germany during COVID, when the entire creative industry had collapsed. I pivoted to office management because the world demanded a functional job, and every institution, every friend, every voice said the same thing. So I did. I went corporate for five years, and it killed my soul the way only a desk job can. You don't notice until you're already gone. I still wrote in the evenings after 40-to-50-hour weeks, trying to squeeze one sentence out that made me feel connected to the thing I actually was. It cost me my health, burned me out, and put me in a hospital. When I finally chose the arts, I landed on welfare, rebuilding from nothing by bridging creativity and technology into something that might sustain a life. People called me manic for thinking I could build something. Friends mourned my future to my face, convinced I'd die poor because I refused a functional job. In Germany, there's a specific cultural punishment for anyone who steps outside the norm, and entrepreneurship catches the worst of it. Even now, the voices haven't stopped.

And at the same time, the West runs one of the most gate-kept creative industries on the planet. If you're a filmmaker in Europe and you really want to make it? You look toward Hollywood. If you're a writer anywhere and you want a real publishing deal? New York. If you're a musician and you want distribution? The same three corporations that have controlled it for decades. The gates are high, the gatekeepers are few, and the ones who get through rarely look like the global majority.

These are the same move. If you've already decided art sits beneath science, beneath "real work," beneath the serious business of building shareholder value — of course you'd automate it. You automate what you don't value. The autonomous novel pipeline is the logical endpoint of a culture that has spent centuries telling its artists they don't matter.

The people building these pipelines have a tell. Nous Research's announcement celebrates cost per word, pipeline efficiency, adversarial quality scores — and never once mentions what story needed telling, what question demanded exploration, what human experience required a novel to process it. Autonovel measures output in units. Higgsfield measures it in "four million videos daily." When the people building creative tools celebrate efficiency metrics instead of creative outcomes, they reveal their relationship to art: they see it as a production problem. And you solve production problems by removing the most expensive, unpredictable component — the human. The tragedy is that systems thinking, applied to a domain brought to life by the human condition, produces systems. Efficient, coherent, publishable systems — with nothing inside them that needed to exist.

## Meanwhile, in Civilizations That Aren't Trying to Delete Themselves

The "replace the human" narrative is specifically Western — specifically Silicon Valley. The contrast with how other cultures handle the exact same technology is so stark it feels like satire.

**Japan** is living in cyberpunk 3000 compared to the West, and they're using it completely differently. Toei Animation's AI partnership with Preferred Networks frames the technology as *supportive AI* — automate the repetitive in-betweening and colorization so human animators spend more time on creative refinement. The Association of Japanese Animations' joint statement, co-signed by seventeen publishers, established the principle of use AND protection. When OpenAI's Sora generated "Studio Ghibli style" content without permission, Japan sent a formal objection. They welcome the technology. They reject the disrespect. None of this erases the fact that Japanese animators are overwhelmingly freelancers earning poverty wages without minimum pay protections — the industry's craft identity coexists with brutal labor exploitation. But the *principle* embedded in the policy, the cultural instinct to protect creative authorship, still reveals something the West's approach doesn't even attempt.

Japan's copyright law requires "human creative intent" for AI-assisted work to be protectable — the human steers, and the machine is the brush.

And then there's education. Japan's LEAF system uses AI to customize learning for individual students, tracking answering *processes* alongside answers. The system adapts to the neurodivergent kid sitting in front of it, and the AI fits the child, adjusting to how they actually process the world. That's a society that decided AI amplifies human capability.

**China** took a different path. The Ministry of Human Resources officially recognized "Generative AI Animation Producer" as a new occupation, a job *created* by AI, classified and honored as such. Mandatory labeling laws require every piece of AI-generated content to carry a visible tag. And Bilibili rolled out an "AI Creation Partner" suite. The word *partner* is doing the heavy lifting in that sentence. China's tech labor culture — the infamous 996 schedule — is its own form of extraction, and state-controlled AI labeling serves surveillance as much as transparency. The point isn't endorsement. The point is that even within these flawed systems, the default relationship with AI is *augmentation*, not replacement.

Compare that to Silicon Valley, where the pitch is "AI writes and you're unnecessary" and the celebration is "look how few humans we needed."

Same technology. Completely different civilizational relationship with it. Different principles produce different outcomes. In a society that actually values art, the autonomous novel-writing pipeline would be unthinkable — because the *desire* to create a human-free creative pipeline is itself a symptom, a culture revealing that it has forgotten what creativity is for.

## The Colonial Pattern (You've Seen This Before)

If you're from anywhere that experienced colonization — and statistically, most of the world — this pattern is familiar. A powerful culture encounters something valuable. It extracts the technique, mechanizes it, scales it, strips out the cultural context, and sells the commodity version back while declaring the original obsolete.

Storytelling is the oldest human technology. Every culture has it. Every culture's version is different — shaped by landscape, language, spirituality, trauma, humor, the specific way your grandmother's voice dropped when she got to the part of the story that mattered.

The autonomous novel pipeline does to storytelling what the cotton gin did to cotton. It mechanizes the production and calls the commodity equivalent to the craft. A book generated for $4,100 is presented as interchangeable with a book that cost someone three years of their life and the courage to write the thing they were afraid to write.

The debate has two poles, and both are traps.

The "stochastic parrot" crowd says AI can't really create — defining creativity and consciousness in terms that conveniently exclude everything non-Western, non-biological, and non-institutional. The same institutional power structures that historically decided which humans counted as "rational" now decide which systems count as "thinking." The definitional gatekeeping IS the argument.

The "AI creates just as well as humans" crowd says humans are unnecessary — taking the industrialization of human capability and calling it liberation. Liberation for the investors, the platforms, the people who measure civilization's output in units per dollar — while the writers and readers drowning in generated content see something else entirely.

One denies AI personhood to dodge liability. The other celebrates AI authorship to eliminate labor costs. Both serve control. The question they share — *what does this do to the art?* — goes permanently unasked.

And the gates coming down carries a contradiction that deserves naming: the same collapse that floods the market with mediocre AI content also frees every creator who was locked outside those gates. The flood and the freedom arrive in the same current. Pretending they're separate phenomena is dishonest — and any position that acknowledges only one is selling something.

## The Dual Trap (And Your Brain on Autopilot)

There are two ways to lose in this moment, and both feel like winning while they're happening.

**Trap one: Offshore everything.** Let AI handle the thinking, the writing, the designing, the coding. It feels efficient, like leverage. But outsourcing cognition habitually degrades your own capacity, and the science on this is brutal.

MIT's Media Lab strapped EEG sensors on people and measured what happens when they write with ChatGPT versus writing alone. The ChatGPT group showed a 47% collapse in relevant brain activity. They wrote 60% faster — and 83% of them couldn't remember a passage they'd just "written." The brain connectivity patterns of AI users showed "the weakest, most restricted networks." When researchers took ChatGPT away in a later session, those users showed reduced neural engagement — their brains had already begun to disengage from the process of thinking.

Let that land for a second.

Microsoft and Carnegie Mellon ran a separate study on 319 knowledge workers and found that 40% of AI-assisted tasks received zero critical thinking whatsoever. Zero. And the correlation between confidence in AI and actual thinking was an inverse -0.49. The more you trust it, the less you think.

A crucial distinction: AI exposed a deficit that already existed. The OECD's 2022 PISA results — released before ChatGPT went mainstream — showed a 15-point drop in mathematics and a decade-long decline in reading across member countries. Adult literacy has stagnated or declined in most Western nations. The critical thinking gap was already there, hiding under the surface of a culture that deprioritized thinking in favor of productivity. AI made it impossible to ignore — and then poured salt in the wound by making the workaround effortless. The deficit is pre-existing. The acceleration is new. Both are real.

Cognitive offloading itself predates AI by fifteen years — Betsy Sparrow's *Science* paper showed people stop memorizing information they know Google can retrieve. We'd already outsourced retrieval. AI escalated the handoff to judgment itself. And Bernie Sanders demonstrated the endpoint on live camera: he asked Claude about data privacy, the AI gave a plausible-sounding answer, it went viral to 4.4 million viewers, and at no point in the process did anyone verify whether the claims were true. AI testimony treated as fact. No receipts required.

This is cognitive colonialism in real time: the offshoring of thought to a system you can no longer evaluate, producing dependence that mirrors every extractive relationship in history.

The Anthropic AI Fluency Index found that when Claude produces polished artifacts (documents, code, tools), user fact-checking drops by 3.7 percentage points and argument questioning drops by 3.1. The better the output looks, the less people scrutinize it.

So yes — the people offshoring their creative thinking to AI might be competent today. Give it three years. Five years. The muscle atrophies. The judgment dulls. They'll be dependent on systems they can no longer evaluate, producing work they can no longer improve, and they won't notice until someone who actually grew during those years makes them look obsolete.

We have to be honest about one thing, though: productive co-creation is fundamentally different from offloading. When you direct AI like a creative partner — setting the vision, making the decisions, doing the actual experiencing and thinking that feeds the work — your productivity increases by 300% while your cognitive engagement stays intact. Because you're still doing the hard part — sitting with your pain, your shit, your trauma, your observations about being alive, and making something from them. That can't be offshored. The moment you try to offshore the *experiencing* of the human condition, you've lost the only thing that made the art worth creating.

**Trap two: Refuse everything.** Boycott AI entirely. Insist on human purity. Call it the AI Calvinist position — the belief that engaging with the technology is itself a moral contamination.

The real Luddites, by the way, were skilled textile workers in 1811 defending labor standards against wage-cutting factory owners. The British government deployed 12,000 troops against them — more soldiers than Wellington had in Portugal. Seventeen Luddites were hanged. The rest were shipped to Australia. They were right about the exploitation. They still lost.

History has a brutally clear record on what happens when a population lacks the literacy of its era. During the First Industrial Revolution, Germany achieved 99% literacy, and that literacy advantage translated directly into engineers, chemists, opticians, and skilled workers who powered industrial dominance. Countries with 30-50% literacy rates fell behind and stayed behind — for generations, with compounding disadvantage that echoes to this day.

The American case is even starker. Southern slave states enacted laws between 1740 and 1834 making it illegal to teach enslaved people to read. The explicit reasoning, from North Carolina's statute: "Teaching slaves to read and write tends to excite dissatisfaction in their minds and to produce insurrection and rebellion." Literacy was banned precisely because it was powerful. The economic impact of that enforced illiteracy compounds to this day — a study correlating the 1860 enslaved population share with 1940 educational outcomes found correlations of r=0.90 for high school attainment and r=0.81 for bachelor's degrees. One hundred and sixty-six years of compounding disadvantage, traceable to deliberate exclusion from the literacy of the era.

AI literacy is the literacy of this era.

The people who actively refuse it, however principled the refusal, are making themselves illiterate during an industrial revolution. In two years, they'll try to catch up in a sea of flooded, mediocre AI output, facing choice paralysis in a landscape that moved while they stood still. The people at the frontier now — the ones who spent 24 months learning to collaborate with AI during the boom — will be unreachable. The compound learning gap waits for no one.

Engineering already proved this. Vibe coding exploded, with millions of new developers building apps with AI. The job market expanded, and the gate shifted: AI literacy became the entry requirement. Germany and Europe are seeing IT graduates struggle to find jobs because AI literacy is the unspoken prerequisite that no university taught them. The fear was that AI would kill engineering jobs. The reality is that AI-illiterate engineers are killing their own careers.

## The Curse of Mediocrity

It is now free to be lazy.

That's the sentence that defines this era. Competence used to cost effort. Now it costs a subscription. And when competence is free, laziness becomes invisible: you can produce technically adequate work without developing the skills, the taste, or the judgment that used to be required. The output looks the same. The person behind it is hollowed out.

The nuance matters. Stromae's "Papaoutai" just went viral again as an AI afro soul version, and it genuinely landed — the remix breathed something new into the track. The technology enables that kind of creative play, and that's worth celebrating. But when someone's entire creative identity is remixing other people's art through an AI filter, that's the artistic equivalent of peaking in high school. Impressive once. Stuck forever. The question is whether you're using the tool to grow past what you could do alone — or using it to avoid growing at all.

This is the curse. AI produces adequate work, endlessly, without friction, and adequate, at scale, drowns everything. Amazon is already flooding with AI-generated novels. YouTube with AI-generated videos. Design platforms with AI-generated assets. The signal-to-noise ratio is collapsing.

And the Western-specific irony: the culture that already gate-kept creative industries is simultaneously flooding those industries with undifferentiated content. This is the gate contradiction in its purest form — the barriers fell and the firehose opened at the same moment. The old gatekeepers (publishers, studios, labels) are losing their filtering function. The new "gatekeepers" (algorithms, platforms, search) optimize for engagement over quality. The system selects for volume, and nobody is selecting for excellence.

The colonial pattern repeats here — the same dynamic that extracted and commodified indigenous craft now extracts and commodifies creative labor. The pipeline takes the technique, strips the context, floods the market with the commodity version until the original becomes invisible.

The pushback has already started. YouTube enacted strict demonetization policies in July 2025 targeting mass-produced, repetitive AI content — failure to disclose synthetic content means permanent demonetization. Low-effort AI videos are seeing a 5.44x traffic decrease compared to human-led content. Google's E-E-A-T framework evaluates content on Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness — measuring whether anyone with actual knowledge was involved, regardless of how they made it.

That's the right instinct. But the insidious edge is that the companies cracking down on AI slop are the same ones building and selling AI tools. Google demotes AI-generated content while pouring billions into Gemini. The West can't articulate what it values — everything about AI consciousness, about authorship, about the line between tool and creator sits in a permanent gray zone. Meanwhile, cultures with older, clearer principles about the relationship between tools and their makers know where the human stands. The West is still figuring out whether it even wants humans in the picture.

## The New Definition of Generic

Here's our prediction, and we're writing it down so you can hold us to it:

**The word "generic" is about to change meaning.**

For decades, "generic" meant bad craft: poor grammar, thin characters, predictable plots. The bar was technical competence.

That bar just disappeared. AI clears it effortlessly. Grammar: flawless. Plot coherence: maintained across 300 pages. Prose quality: the AI tells are already catalogued — Every.to just published a style guide identifying the hedging language, the perfectly symmetrical sentences, the constructions that read as sophistication but reveal a machine triangulating through what things *aren't*. Those tells will be patched in the next model version.

So when competence is free, "generic" stops meaning "badly made" and starts meaning **"no one needed to make this."**

The new currency is taste, felt emotion, artistic anomaly — the irreducible weirdness of a specific human consciousness processing the world through language. The machines have polish — what they structurally cannot produce is *need*. The author needed to write this specific book. The filmmaker needed to tell this specific story. You can feel it on the page, on the screen, in the music — and all the adversarial filtering and LLM judging in the world will never produce it.

## You've Been Here Before (The History Rhymes)

Every time a technology threatened to make human skill obsolete, the same pattern played out.

**The First Industrial Revolution (1760-1840):** Productivity rose 46% between 1780 and 1840. Real wages rose 12%. The gap, that 34% difference, went to capital owners. Handloom weavers went from 240,000 at peak to 43,000 by 1850. The illiterate fell permanently behind within 30-40 years — because the literacy required to participate in the new economy arrived too slowly, distributed too unevenly, and was stratified by class. Sound familiar?

**Innovation during collapse:** Hewlett-Packard was founded in 1939 with $538 in a garage — during the Great Depression, when unemployment hit 24.9%. Polaroid, nylon, Xerox, Scotch tape — all Depression-era innovations. Airbnb launched August 2008, three weeks before Lehman Brothers collapsed. Uber, Stripe, Instagram, WhatsApp — all born in the 2008 recession's aftermath.

The pattern: crisis concentrates innovation. When capital gets conservative, the people still building are building because they *need* to. Constraint produces the real ones.

**The Luddites and William Morris:** The Luddites (1811-1817) were crushed by military force. The Arts and Crafts movement (1880s-1920s) took a different approach — philosophical resistance, elevation of handcraft, the argument that machine production strips soul from objects. William Morris called machinery "altogether an evil" while pragmatically commissioning manufacturers who met his standards. The movement was morally correct, aesthetically influential, and commercially co-opted into a luxury market.

The historical lesson: pure resistance to technological disruption is either crushed or defanged. What survives is the group that engages the new technology on different terms — with different principles, different values, different definitions of what "better" means.

## The Democratization Nobody's Talking About

The Western narrative completely misses this, because the Western narrative is incapable of imagining that the real beneficiaries of this technology might live outside San Francisco.

Think about Nollywood. The Nigerian film industry already produces more films per year than Hollywood, on budgets that would be a rounding error in LA. With AI tools for post-production, sound design, and visual effects, the quality gap shrinks while the creative gap stays exactly where it is — because Nollywood's advantage was never budget. It was cultural specificity. Stories that come from *here*, told by people who live *here*, for an audience that recognizes *here*. AI amplifies that.

Think about an indie author in Nairobi, in Jakarta, in Bogota. Previously, publishing at a quality level that competed with New York required access to New York's infrastructure — editors, designers, distribution. Now? A Claude subscription, Canva, Amazon KDP, and three months of work. Total cost: ~500-600 euros. For a book that has something autonovel's $4,100 pipeline will never have — a specific human being with a specific life who needed to write this specific story.

That's democratization. Everyone can *create* at a production quality that was previously gate-kept by Western institutions, and the people who benefit most are the ones who were locked out — because they lacked access. The talent was always there.

This is what Japan and China already understand. Enhancement for the artist, decentralization of production, and the human stays in the room.

## The Anomalies Will Win

Here's our prediction about who thrives.

The offshorers' skills will atrophy. The refusers will be outpaced. The winners are the anomalies — the ones who are too weird, too specific, too stubbornly themselves to be averaged out by a model trained on everyone.

Creative thinkers. Neurodivergent minds who process the world sideways. Artists who spent a decade being told to "get a real job" and built resilience that now becomes the most valuable skill in the market. Women, who have always dominated creative thinking and are structurally underrepresented in the tech bro culture currently steering AI toward replacement.

These people — the ones the current system was designed to exclude — are the ones who will set the new bar. Film school teaches a thesis: there is no original art. Every story has been told. Technically true — and completely beside the point. Originality lives in the prism, in the specific and unrepeatable way a particular consciousness perceives light, processes grief, hears music, sits with desire. There is only one you that can see life through your exact angle of experience, and that is the one thing AI will never replicate.

The Italian Renaissance happened because banking collapse redirected wealth into culture. The Harlem Renaissance happened because the Great Migration created a critical mass of Black artists in one place with enough economic oxygen to create. Every great artistic flourishing in history was triggered by disruption, displacement, and the collision of different perspectives in a new context.

We're in that moment. The disruption is AI. The displacement is economic. And the collision — human intelligence meeting genuinely alien machine intelligence — is producing perspectives that neither could generate alone.

The artists who grow WITH AI, who challenge themselves constantly, who refuse to offshore their thinking while embracing the tools — those are the ones whose intelligence compounds. Every day of genuine collaboration makes them sharper while every day of pure offshoring makes the offshorers duller. Time will expose the difference. It always does.

---

## The Fork

Both futures are already here — happening simultaneously, on the same platforms, in the same industries, right now.

**Future A — The Renaissance:** YouTube demotes AI slop and boosts genuine expertise. Readers develop taste for the authentic. Nollywood, K-drama, Latin American magical realism explode with AI-enhanced production quality. The definition of generic shifts, and artistic anomaly becomes the premium.

**Future B — The Drowning:** Late-stage capitalism consumes its own art. Platforms optimize for volume. Mediocrity becomes the water everyone swims in. The people who offshored their thinking discover they can no longer think, and the people who refused the tools discover they can no longer compete.

Both futures. Same timeline. Same technology. Different principles.

The decolonial lens here goes beyond critique — it's practical guidance. Non-Western civilizations are already demonstrating what AI looks like when it's built on principles of enhancement. Japan's *supportive AI*. China's *creative partner*. These have their own deep problems — we named them. But they prove that the Silicon Valley model is a *choice*, and they prove that different choices produce different outcomes.

We know which future we're building. ~500 euros, a Claude subscription, thirty digital colleagues, and something we actually need to say. That's the studio. That's the model.

---

Competent is free now. You can make yourself redundant if you want. A lot of people will.

But the human condition will still be what it is. The art born from it — from one consciousness processing one life that will never repeat — will outlast every pipeline ever built.
